## Free vs paid QR code generators

Most paid QR code generators charge for three things: dynamic QR codes, analytics, and bulk generation. Here's my take on each.

**Dynamic QR codes** let you change the destination URL after printing. The QR code points to a redirect server, and you update where it goes from a dashboard. This sounds useful, but it means your QR code depends on someone else's server staying online. If that company shuts down or changes pricing, your printed codes stop working. Static QR codes encode the data directly, so they work forever with no dependencies.

**Analytics** means the QR code routes through a tracking server that counts scans. If you need scan counts, you can get the same data by using a UTM-tagged URL and checking your own analytics tool. You don't need to pay a QR code company for that.

**Bulk generation** is something we handle for free. The [bulk vCard tool](/vcard/bulk) processes CSV files, and the CLI can loop through any list. There's a full walkthrough in the [bulk QR code generation](/blog/bulk-qr-code-generation) post.

The features that actually matter for most people are customization, multiple formats, and clean exports. Those are all free here.

## Print guidelines

QR codes that look fine on screen sometimes fail when printed. A few rules will save you from wasted prints.

**Minimum size.** Keep your QR code at least 2 cm (about 0.8 inches) on each side. Smaller than that and phone cameras may struggle to focus, especially in low light. If the code encodes a lot of data (vCards with addresses, for example), it will have more modules and need to be larger. For a vCard, I wouldn't go below 3 cm.

**Quiet zone.** The white space around the QR code isn't decoration. Scanners need it to find the edges of the code. Leave at least 4 modules of blank space on each side. The generator handles this automatically in the export, but don't crop it away in your design tool.

**Contrast.** Dark modules on a light background works best. You can use colors, but make sure there's enough contrast. A dark blue on white is fine. A light yellow on white is not. I go into this more in the [design best practices post](/blog/qr-code-design-best-practices).

**File format.** Use SVG for anything that will be printed. It's vector-based, so it stays sharp at any size. PNG works for screens but will look pixelated if you scale it up for a poster. If your print shop asks for a PDF, you can open the SVG in any design tool and export to PDF without losing quality.

**Test before printing.** Scan the code with at least two different phones before sending anything to print. Test it on the actual printed material if you can. A code that works on your monitor might fail on a dark-colored paper stock.

## PNG vs SVG

You always get both formats. Which one you pick depends on where the QR code is going.

PNG works well for screens: social media posts, presentations, email signatures, messaging apps. It's a raster image, so it has a fixed resolution.

SVG is what you want for print. Business cards, flyers, posters, packaging. It's vector-based, meaning it stays sharp whether you print it at 1 inch or 10 feet. It also opens cleanly in Figma, Illustrator, and other design tools if you need to drop it into a layout.

If you're unsure, grab both. They're free.
